Visibility of System Status

ObservationAdd real-time order tracking status updates to improve feedback during the delivery process.

User Control & Freedom

Consistency & Standards
Some button styles and icon usage are inconsistent across different screens, which may cause confusion for first-time users.
RecommendationEstablish a unified design system with consistent button sizes, colors, and icon styles across all screens.
5
Major Issue
Error Prevention
The checkout form lacks sufficient validation and confirmation prompts, allowing users to submit incomplete or incorrect orders without adequate warning.
RecommendationImplement inline form validation, required field indicators, and an order review/confirmation screen before final submission.
6
Minor Issue
Recognition Rather Than Recall
Users must remember previously viewed medicines when comparing options, as there is no persistent comparison or recently-viewed feature.
RecommendationAdd a "Recently Viewed" section and a product comparison feature to reduce memory load.

No Issue

The search and filter functionality allows experienced users to quickly find specific medicines, supporting efficient task completion.

Some product pages contain excessive text and competing visual elements that distract from the primary action of adding items to the cart.

Summary

Key Issues Identified

The most critical usability problems discovered through both heuristic evaluation and usability testing sessions.

  • 1
    Insufficient Error Prevention at Checkout
    Users were able to proceed through the checkout flow with missing required fields, leading to failed submissions and frustration. No inline validation or confirmation step existed before the final order was placed.
    Heuristic #5 — Error Prevention
    High
  • 2
    Unclear and Generic Error Messages
    When errors occurred (e.g., out-of-stock items, failed search), the system displayed vague messages such as "Something went wrong" without indicating the cause or how to recover.
    Heuristic #9 — Error Recovery
    High
  • 3
    Inconsistent UI Components Across Screens
    Different button styles and icon treatments were observed across the home screen, product listing, and cart pages, reducing the perceived professionalism of the interface and slowing user navigation.
    Heuristic #4 — Consistency & Standards
    Medium
  • 4
    Medical Terminology Unfamiliar to General Users
    Category labels such as "Analgesics," "Antipyretics," and "Antihypertensives" were difficult for non-medical users to interpret, causing navigation confusion during task completion.
    Heuristic #2 — Match with Real World
    Medium
  • 5
    Cluttered Product Detail Pages
    Product pages contained too much information presented without clear visual hierarchy, making it difficult for users to quickly identify the primary call-to-action (Add to Cart button).
    Heuristic #8 — Aesthetic & Minimalist Design
    Medium
Discussion

Discussion of Findings

The usability evaluation of the MediQuick prototype revealed a system that is generally functional and meets the basic expectations of an online pharmacy platform. The overall SUS score of 77.5 places the prototype in the "Good" range, indicating that users found the system acceptable and reasonably easy to use for common tasks.

However, the heuristic evaluation surfaced several recurring issues that, if left unaddressed, could significantly impact the experience of non-technical users — particularly those with limited digital literacy or unfamiliarity with medical terminology.

The two most critical issues — insufficient error prevention and unclear error messaging — represent fundamental gaps in the current prototype's ability to guide users through failure scenarios. These findings are consistent with usability literature suggesting that error recovery support is one of the most impactful factors in user satisfaction.

"Users are not the problem. The design is the problem." — Don Norman, The Design of Everyday Things

Positive findings include strong scores on learnability (Q7) and perceived frequency of use (Q1), suggesting that first-time users found the overall concept compelling and the core navigation model intuitive. This is a strong foundation upon which targeted improvements can be built.
